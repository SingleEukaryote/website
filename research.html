PhD Student @ amoeba.msstate.edu

Viewing the micro-world is eye opening for many people — both young and old. A central mission in my life is to collect, visualize, and share stories from the micro-level to ignite more curiosity about the world. I hope to show throughout my work that protists are charismatic systems for storytelling (education) and story-chasing (research).

Studying non-model organisms will bring new insights to the field of biology.

I study protists for this reason, as they represent a vast and evolutionarily intruiging group of life. The more we study them, the more we uncover exceptions to the standard models of biology taught in classrooms. From discovering new species and supergroups to observing unique cell behaviors, symbioses, and unusual genetics, protists offer immense potential for discovery-base research. My research explores the cell biology and behaviors of non-model protists, with an emphasis on those that exhibit emergent collective behavior.

THE ORB


Protists, or microbial eukaryotes, play vital roles in various habitats, including aquatic and terrestrial environments as well as animal microbiomes. Their diverse range of relationships includes parasitic and commensal interactions, as well as free-living roles in driving nutrient cycles and stabilizing the biosphere. As the most understudied yet phylogenetically diverse group of eukaryotic life, protists offer a wealth of opportunities to uncover novel biological processes, redefine fundamental aspects of cell biology, and address intriguing evolutionary questions.
